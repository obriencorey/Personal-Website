Cycloidal Gearbox II

January 15th, 2021 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania


Back with the cycloidal gearboxes! I had a number of improvements to make, so let's run through them. First, the most obvious improvement was to make it an actual gearbox and not some fidget toy that you needed to hold together with your hands to actually use. We can check that box, as this is a pretty solid unit. Next, I needed some way to input torque into the gearbox without using external hardware. I designed a simple little crank which you can see in the Fusion 360 design preview box below.


Additionally, I wanted to ensure you could assemble the gearbox without needing any hardware. I thought about making 3D printed snapping connectors but decided that would be too weak. Seeing as my main goal with this project was to demonstrate how cool the mechanism is, I just made the case threaded. Let this be a warning, do not connect this to your car to gain more torque. Very danger.

A CNC Carved Volcano Incense Burner

January 8th, 2021 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania


I just think topographic maps are the coolest! And you should really look up how satellites get this data. I then designed the model in Fusion 360 and made it! I can't believe a computer can do this kind of stuff.

Cycloidal Gearbox

January 5th, 2021 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


I don't have much to say about this one, since it more or less speaks for itself. These gearboxes are just plain cool! The gear reduction is 1 : N where N is the number of pins you have on the outside ring. I used these shelf pins I had laying around, so I ended up with 20 pins. 1:20 gearbox is quite nice, and the way it works is mesmerizing.
If you haven't seen how they work, I highly recommend searching up a video. I 3D printed mine and it works great. I have some plans to make an adapter so that it will fit a NEMA17 stepper motor. I don't have any plans as to what I would do after that, but a high torque, low backlash gearbox could come in handy one day!

Simulating Complex Spring Mass System

July 25th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


I wrote this program using a combination of MATLAB and Processing. The program used a Runge-Kutta approximation to solve a system of complex differential equations which described the equations of motion for the picture spring mass system.
This small project was an extension of an assignment for one of the classes I took over the summer. We were simply asked to produce the graph shown but I figured why let such good data go to waste! I took that data and put it into Processing where I used it to animate the system. And it looks great! You can see the initial position and the final position shown.

Calculating the Specific Impulse of Homemade Rocket Motors

July 9th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


Finally! A way to measure and verify my rocket motors are making any worthwhile thrust. The goal of this project was to create a test stand which would give me a time vs thrust graph of the rocket motor, so I could calculate specific impulse. This calculation is trivial with such a relationship and the weight of the propellant.
And trivial it was! I was able to get quite reasonable numbers from my test stand. I made use of this luggage scale in the lever arrangement as shown. This increased the resolution of my data (i.e. if the scale could measure .1 lb increments and the lever had an advantage of 2, then really the scale would measure .05lb increments) and simplifed the build. I considered using a strain gauge, but figured the electronics would be too delicate for such a task. My motors worked! I made real working rocket motors from nothing more than stump remover and sugar.

The Messenger (ESP8266 based messaging service)

June 12th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


Do you ever want to communicate with the feeling of writing a letter, but the urgency of sending a text? Probably not. But you have to admit this project is super cool! With COVID still looming into the summer, I wanted to invent a new way to communicate with my girlfriend. We were more than 2,000 miles away mind you, so anything to reduce the feeling of that distance would be great!
So what I came up with is a messenging website that works with an ESP8266 microcontroller. First, the message is input on a subdomain of this website. Then it is sent to a secret storage space which the ESP8266 can access. In between sending the message and accessing it, the message is encrypted with a laughable encryption algorithm. It was easy to write in both PHP and C++, so I'm not complaining. Then it is displayed on an Eink display. A nice blue LED flashes when there is a new message and a button is used to dismiss. Overall, it works great! I am so happy with how it turned out!

Controlling a Four Digit Seven Segment Display

April 20th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


Here is a project born out pure laziness. When you control one of these four digit seven segment displays, you are supposed to use a shift register so that you can sequentially write data to each digit while making sure only one digit is lit up. This process is similar to multiplexing and is quite mesmerizing when you really understand how it works. But I don't have a shift register. Nor do I feel a project of mine should be limited by its absence. So I thought I would bootleg one using an ESP microcontoller.

Instead of using a shift register, I just had the ESP turn on and off each digit until I got the same effect. And it works great! Granted, I need 7 + N number of pins for N digits, instead of the 8 I would need with a shift register, but I think imporivsing is an art. And I am an artist.

Designing a Monolithic Pill Container

March 28th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


Look around us. Everything we see, touch, interact with, depend on, has been designed. Meaning someone took the idea they had in their head, combined it with the constraints of the real world, and produced something that we humans use with no second thought. Even objects as simple as forks have such deep roots in product design. I mean, why do they have four tines instead of five? Tangent aside, I wanted to try my hand at improving a basic everyday item: A pill container.

Lucky for me, my constraints were relatively simple. Hold pills. Don't let the pills go. Have slots for each day of the week, and each half of the day if I so choose. My main goal with this design was to make it one piece or monolithic. This would greatly reduce manufacturing time and eliminate the need for assembly. 3D printing is obviously not the manufacturing workhorse I'm pretending it is, but sometimes its fun to pretend and run with it. Plus, if a technology like SLA improves, this pill container could be manufactured rapidly (SLA print times only depend on the height of the object. Since this is relatively small in height, so is the time to manufacture).

My First SLA Print!

September 6th, 2019 (West Lafayette, Indiana)


This is a big day for me! I finally branched out and investigated a new form of 3D printing. StereoLithography Apparatus is pretty cool, a vat of resin is exposed to UV radiation which selectively cures different parts of the resin. Instead of the resolution depending on the size of the nozzle, the resolution here depends on a screen, similar to the screen you are viewing this website on. For this reason, SLA prints come out much cleaner at smaller sizes.

This isn't as involved as my other projects, but it was too cool not to share. I mean look how far technology has come! Even SLA printers are starting to become a desktop commodity. The only issue is that the resin is fairly toxic and gross. You really don't want this stuff getting on your hands. But when you have the facilities, you can make some really great prints, just like this one!

I mean its easy to tell when the design of an object is top priority to the company selling it and we often say things are "well-engineered" but I have never seen something so well designed! The app that you use to control and view the drone with is simple, contains everything you need to fly and create aerial works of art, and its not cluttered as I would expect.

I am writing this after I got the drone, so I can already speak about how well it performs. Did you know that if it loses signal, it gains altitude (Up until 400 feet agl) and flies back to its launch point. I mean what a genius piece of software to implement. That wouldn't even be hard to code, and yet I have a feeling it is a minute detail many of DJI's competitors would overlook. This thing rocks! And I can't wait to see where else I will take it.

Fighter Jet Tiles

November 12th, 2017 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


Pretty simple, but fun. I had some old tiles and some pieces of rock and glass that I needed something to do with. I thought what would be better than a fighter jet mosaic! A perfect combination of art and reuse, I figured I could always cover the tiles in expoxy and make some coasters as well. I started off with two interesting planes, the F-35 and the Eurofighter Typhoon. I sketched them on the tile and then found some pieces to compose the aircraft.


They turned out really well! I only had a few pieces to make the jet exhuast, so I had to improvise. Using the small glass beads as cockpits was also a nice touch, mixing up the materials and bringing it all together.

Making a Terrarium with a Light Bulb

May 6th, 2017 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


I have always liked terrariums. There is something so cool about a mini self supporting ecosystem that you can hold in your hand. That being said, a terrarium is quite difficult without the proper equipment and knowledge. Neither of which I had when I made this one. But fear not, this terrarium lasted nearly a year before suddenly dying. I'm sure it was something like the nitrogen in the soil or some other small detail which was easy to overlook. I never claimed to know what I'm doing!


I even had a little worm which was stuck in the soil I added! I'm not sure how the little guy did, but I'm sure he was having the time of his life being one of few relatively large organisms in there. I find it interesting just how simultaneously fragile and durable life is. Some fish die because there isn't enough salt in the water but this worm lived for nearly a year in a lightbulb. Odd.

Launching a 98% Homemade Model Rocket!

July 12th, 2016 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


Aweeee yeah, another model rocket. I can't even tell you how many I launched prior to this one, or how many I launched after this one. Either way, this one was awesome. It used 2 estes C motors, ripping of the pad with all its thrust. I also spray painted it with a combination of grays, giving it a cool gunmetal look.

Did I mention I made almost all of it from scratch? Everything but the motors were scavenged from my house. From the trash bag parachute to the paper towel tube motor mounts, everything was repurposed and improvised. And it turned out great! Turns out you don't need the most expensive rocket parts in the world to make something that flies great.
