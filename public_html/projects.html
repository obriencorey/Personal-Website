A CNC Carved Volcano Incense Burner

January 4th, 2021 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania


I just think topographic maps are the coolest! And you should really look up how satellites get this data. Isn't it awesome that I was able to generate a model of some part of the earth from some free online data? What a time to be alive. This project began after being inspired by some instagram video I saw a while back. Someone made their version on a much better CNC with better materials, so I knew I had to step it up.


My big change would be an incredible level of detail. I exported the height data at the highest resolution I could and used an absolutely tiny carving bit (0.6 mm, just a little bit than a 3D printer nozzle!). It came out looking just remarkable. The best part was this project began life as an old 2 by 4 in my dad's garage. I used the CNC to plane one side, then squared up both pieces which left me with some great wood to work with. I then designed the model in Fusion 360 and made it! I can't believe a computer can do this kind of stuff.

Displaying the Time With a Single Seven Segment Display

May 19th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


The goal for this project was to use the asbolute smallest amount of hardware to build a clock. Sounds silly, but I thought using a single seven segment display would give me the challenge I was looking for. Plus they are just so simple! Seven LEDs organized to form 10 different digits. All universally understood regardless of langauge. Perhaps I am being overdramatic but those are some important and well designed LEDs.

My plan was to use an ESP8266 microcontroller to access the time from the internet. Sure, you could buy a realtime clock module for the arduino, but my goal was minimalism! I made a little animation that played everytime the time was about to be displayed, digit for digit. This sequence ran through until the ESP was able to access the internet to get the current time again. It works extremely well! I honestly think this could make for a cool futuristic watch. Although I would replace the seven segment since it looks a bit hacked together. Maybe I'll launch my own brand of watches. I'll call it Watch & Learn (Get it? You're watching the watch and learning the time? Okay maybe I'll find another company name).

Controlling a Four Digit Seven Segment Display

April 20th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


Here is a project born out pure laziness. When you control one of these four digit seven segment displays, you are supposed to use a shift register so that you can sequentially write data to each digit while making sure only one digit is lit up. This process is similar to multiplexing and is quite mesmerizing when you really understand how it works. But I don't have a shift register. Nor do I feel a project of mine should be limited by its absence. So I thought I would bootleg one using an ESP microcontoller.

Instead of using a shift register, I just had the ESP turn on and off each digit until I got the same effect. And it works great! Granted, I need 7 + N number of pins for N digits, instead of the 8 I would need with a shift register, but I think imporivsing is an art. And I am an artist.

Creating My Own Tensegrity Structure (Portable Edition)

April 12th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


I'm not entirely sure when it happened, but I became obsessed with tensegrity structures. Especially 3D printed ones. After observing their relatively simple nature, I decided to try to make my own. My design came with one key stipulation though: It needed to be portable. I wanted to mail this print across the country so I needed to ensure it could fit in an envelope. Don't get me wrong, I would have loved to make a giant, chair sized tensegrity structure and mail it out in a wooden crate. But I like a challenge.

The central tower in the middle of my triangular design merely slots in place (adding another level to its tensile integrity). I then connected everything with some lovely blue twine, completing the structure. A giant one I find online is pictured in the back while my own work of art is in the foreground. I also added a fractal pattern to each base because, well, I can. It is 3D printing. The build volume's the limit!

Designing a Monolithic Pill Container

March 28th, 2020 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania)


Look around us. Everything we see, touch, interact with, depend on, has been designed. Meaning someone took the idea they had in their head, combined it with the constraints of the real world, and produced something that we humans use with no second thought. Even objects as simple as forks have such deep roots in product design. I mean, why do they have four tines instead of five? Tangent aside, I wanted to try my hand at improving a basic everyday item: A pill container.

Lucky for me, my constraints were relatively simple. Hold pills. Don't let the pills go. Have slots for each day of the week, and each half of the day if I so choose. My main goal with this design was to make it one piece or monolithic. This would greatly reduce manufacturing time and eliminate the need for assembly. 3D printing is obviously not the manufacturing workhorse I'm pretending it is, but sometimes its fun to pretend and run with it. Plus, if a technology like SLA improves, this pill container could be manufactured rapidly (SLA print times only depend on the height of the object. Since this is relatively small in height, so is the time to manufacture).

My First SLA Print!

September 6th, 2019 (West Lafayette, Indiana)


This is a big day for me! I finally branched out and investigated a new form of 3D printing. StereoLithography Apparatus is pretty cool, a vat of resin is exposed to UV radiation which selectively cures different parts of the resin. Instead of the resolution depending on the size of the nozzle, the resolution here depends on a screen, similar to the screen you are viewing this website on. For this reason, SLA prints come out much cleaner at smaller sizes.

This isn't as involved as my other projects, but it was too cool not to share. I mean look how far technology has come! Even SLA printers are starting to become a desktop commodity. The only issue is that the resin is fairly toxic and gross. You really don't want this stuff getting on your hands. But when you have the facilities, you can make some really great prints, just like this one!

Making a Terrarium with a Light Bulb

May 6th, 2017 (Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania


I have always liked terrariums. There is something so cool about a mini self supporting ecosystem that you can hold in your hand. That being said, a terrarium is quite difficult without the proper equipment and knowledge. Neither of which I had when I made this one. But fear not, this terrarium lasted nearly a year before suddenly dying. I'm sure it was something like the nitrogen in the soil or some other small detail which was easy to overlook. I never claimed to know what I'm doing!


I even had a little worm which was stuck in the soil I added! I'm not sure how the little guy did, but I'm sure he was having the time of his life being one of few relatively large organisms in there. I find it interesting just how simultaneously fragile and durable life is. Some fish die because there isn't enough salt in the water but this worm lived for nearly a year in a lightbulb. Odd.
